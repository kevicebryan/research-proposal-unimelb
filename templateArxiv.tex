\section{Introduction}

The education landscape has evolved tremendously ever since 
ChatGPT was released by OpenAI in November 2022 \cite{sallai2024approach}. 
This was driven by Generative AI's capability in providing personalized feedback based on
students' unique needs \cite{wenjuan2024who}.
Consequently, schools that were initially restrictive and banned the use of AI 
have now embraced it \cite{prather2024beyond}. This transition was led 
by AI being able to help students focus more on creativity and critical thinking 
rather than repetitive tasks \cite{xu2026integrating,qian2025pedagogical}. 
Moreover, the adoption of GenAI has now become a necessity due to the rapidly evolving 
professional landscape \cite{ma2025preparing}. Hence, institutions 
should create an environment that allows AI collaboration and aims to enhance 
human intelligence rather than replace it \cite{sallai2024approach,qian2025pedagogical}.

Despite bringing immense benefits, the adoption of GenAI 
in higher education also comes with its own caveat.
As previously mentioned, GenAI can be useful in taking over repetitive tasks \cite{xia2025systematic}, 
but this also leads to "cognitive offloading" 
where students outsource critical cognitive tasks to the AI \cite{xia2025systematic}. 
The pattern of reliance starts from using AI as an information verification tool 
to slowly becoming a content delegation tool, causing students to blindly rely on 
AI to generate the final output \cite{Hou2025}.
It becomes even more concerning as AI creates a "productivity paradox", 
where despite students experiencing increased productivity, 
they lack depth in critical analysis \cite{Wang2024}.
For instance, due to convenience and speed that AI summaries provide, 
students tend to prioritize these summaries over verifying the underlying sources \cite{Loughlin2026}.
Despite the faster drafting process, the final result ended up being lower quality, 
with generic essays and a lack of critical analysis \cite{sallai2024approach,Wang2024}. 
Moreover, this matter is further supported by the so-called "death of essays", 
a phenomenon that has risen to prominence since COVID-19 due to the 
increase in take-home exams and students utilizing AI to cheat and plagiarize \cite{sallai2024approach}.
This raises the importance of AI literacy.
Studies show that students with higher AI literacy tend to take a collaborative approach 
by planning and evaluating AI outputs, which consequently leads to better outcomes compared 
to students with lower AI literacy, who tend to over-rely on AI just to get the job done \cite{KimetAl2026}.
Moreover, over-reliance on AI negatively leads to the decline of face-to-face interactions, which can 
cause implications on student's interpersonal skills and emotional intelligence. Consequently, this 
increases the risk of social anxiety and isolation \cite{KlimovaPikhart2025}.

% Illusion of truth and confidence miscalibration
As previously mentioned, over-reliance on AI is a significant concern 
that must be addressed. AI tends to generate text with a 
convincing tone, giving an illusion that the output is always true, 
causing users to overlook hallucinations and in the end hallucinating themselves \cite{sallai2024approach, Loughlin2026}.
To combat this, students are required to possess foundational 
knowledge before adopting the tool \cite{sallai2024approach, Jin2025}.
This ensures they are able to critically validate the AI's information, 
as the human in the loop is responsible for the final output produced \cite{sallai2024approach,Lo2023}. 
Therefore, students must be taught to verify AI outputs \cite{Mollick2023}. 
On the other hand, educators are encouraged to create critical thinking assessments 
that can't be replicated by AI, such as video presentations or pitches \cite{Mollick2023,sallai2024approach}.

As previously mentioned, AI tutoring styles can be made more dynamic \cite{wenjuan2024who}. 
The characteristics of the AI's interaction can be engineered 
using prompts (prompt engineering) and guardrails \cite{Yazici2024}. 
In other words, poor learning outcomes are not solely based on the AI's accuracy 
and performance, but also an issue with the interaction design itself \cite{Lyu2025,Bastani2025}. 
This creates a strong opportunity in designing ways students can interact with AI.

% The need of guardrails to combat the illusion of truth
To address the illusion of truth, AI tools must be designed with guardrails that 
encourage cognitive engagement rather than handing out direct answers \cite{Wang2026SRL}.
In the context of learning, the design of the AI interaction itself is more important than its capabilities \cite{Bastani2025}.
Evidence shows that the use of scaffolding techniques, such as guiding hints and follow-up questions, 
are able to significantly improve students' learning outcomes \cite{Bastani2025}.
AI chatbots with these guardrails are referred to as Socratic chatbots, which are designed to prevent giving out 
direct answers to encourage self-reflection and deep understanding of the topic \cite{Jin2025, Hilliger2025, Jabbour2025}.
When compared to default GenAI models, Socratic chatbots are able to produce 79\% more learning 
improvements than the default models (127\% vs. 48\%) during practice assessment \cite{Bastani2025}. 
Additionally, unlike the default model where students experience 
learning loss when AI is removed, 
students did not experience any learning loss when the Socratic chatbot is removed \cite{Bastani2025}. 
The results indicates that despite giving short-term benefits, default AI 
interaction actually causes miscalibration in confidence (over-confidence), which
creates an illusion of competence \cite{Bastani2025}.

It is important to note that even though Socratic tutoring may work 
in many contexts, it is not a one-size-fits-all solution \cite{Liding2026}.
When compared to the default AI in the context of coding, Socratic 
AI tends to produce a higher error rate without a clear indication 
of actually improving the students' long-term learning outcome \cite{Liding2026}. 
Additionally, it's interaction style can be frustrating to 
learners with lower AI literacy \cite{Jin2025}. This amplifies the need 
to explore alternative tutoring styles that could improve both learning outcome  
and confidence calibration, as well as cater to unique students' needs.


% introducing reverse tutoring
Despite the growing evidence that AI-supported learning possess, most studies 
have not evaluate different tutoring styles within a single study. 
As a result, there is a lack of comparative evidence on which style on their 
performance and confidence calibration. 
Beyond Socratic approaches, evidence shows that reverse tutoring 
can be effective in boosting cognitive engagement and knowledge mastery \cite{Okita2013,Fiorella2023,Lyu2025,Wong2026}.
This pedagogical approach embodies the theory of 
learning-by-teaching by using recursive feedback that 
observes how a tutee applies what has been taught \cite{Okita2013}. 
This approach is similar to designs where the AI takes the role of a student, 
prompting the human learner to teach the AI, 
thereby reinforcing the learner’s own understanding \cite{Mollick2023}.
Studies show that reverse tutoring creates the protégé effect 
where students learn harder when they know they must teach \cite{Okita2013,Wong2026}.
There are two mechanisms that align with reverse tutoring \cite{Fiorella2023}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Generative processing: Students are encouraged to inherently organize and integrate their knowledge.
    \item Metacognitive monitoring: Ability to detect explanation gaps, triggering self-correction.
\end{enumerate}
Moreover, these interactions helps drive the learners to 
dig deeper into the topic which leads to a more flexible 
mastery of the topic \cite{Fiorella2023,Lyu2025}.

Hence, in addition to the Socratic tutoring style, 
this study introduces the reverse tutoring technique. 
This study compares the three tutoring styles (direct-answer, Socratic, and reverse tutoring) 
within a single comparative framework, 
while assessing both cognitive and psychological outcomes. 
In particular, reverse tutoring, which is grounded in learning-by-teaching theory, 
despite showing clear effectiveness in improving learning outcomes, 
remains underexplored in the GenAI context.
Therefore, this study aims to fill that gap by asking how different tutoring styles compare in their 
effects on learning performance, knowledge acquisition, self-efficacy, 
and confidence calibration. This combined evaluation is important, as 
prior work shows that students can appear more productive 
with AI, but could lose their learning once the tool is removed \cite{Wang2024,Bastani2025}.
Accordingly, this study aims to determine which tutoring style best supports durable 
learning rather than short-term productivity.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature-review}

\subsection{Generative AI in Education}
It cannot be denied that AI has revolutionized the way 
the education industry operates \cite{ma2025preparing}.
In fact, AI has been implemented within the education industry for decades \cite{Jabbour2025}.
First introduced in 1970, the SCHOLAR tutor system was actually the first 
intelligent tutoring system (ITS) \cite{Mousavinasab2021}. The system was able 
to create a two-way interaction and also evaluate students' knowledge 
by using a semantic network of facts \cite{Mousavinasab2021}. Then, over the 
past decade, we were presented with highly specialized AI tools, such as 
GeoGebra and Wolfram Alpha for math, and also Grammarly for writing \cite{Jabbour2025}. 
Although these tools were effective, they lack the conversational 
capabilities that current GenAI models possess. 
Driven by natural language processing (NLP), 
these large language models (LLMs) are able 
to hold sustained dialogue and have since 
changed the way humans interact with information \cite{Jabbour2025,sallai2024approach}.

The presence of GenAI has not only changed the way students and teachers interact, 
but also the entire curriculum at all levels of education \cite{Mollick2023,prather2024beyond, Alshumaimeri2024}.
From initially banning the use of AI due to concerns about cheating and plagiarism, 
institutions are now embracing and integrating AI into their pedagogy \cite{prather2024beyond, ma2025preparing}.
This shift is supported by GenAI's ability to deliver an adaptive and personalized 
learning experience for students \cite{wenjuan2024who}. 
For a long time, education has been challenged in providing 
for the unique needs of each individual student \cite{Jabbour2025}.
However, the cost of providing human one-on-one tutoring 
is high in both financial and human resources, 
especially as teachers are also burdened with 
administrative responsibilities \cite{Gurung2025,Mollick2023}. 
This is where GenAI comes into play by providing a 
scalable and cost-effective one-on-one tutor compared 
to traditional human tutors, which cost \$3,500--\$4,300 per student per year \cite{Gurung2025}. 
Hence, it lowers the entry barrier for complex subjects by 
providing examples and analogies that cater to the 
user's understanding and interests \cite{prather2024beyond,Leong2024}.
For example, the AI is able to turn a dry vocabulary learning experience by 
catering it's output based on the user's interests such as sports, cooking, or 
sci-fi which consequently boosts the user's motivation \cite{Leong2024,sallai2024approach}. 

Therefore, it is crucial to standardize the use of GenAI in education. 
In aiding that mission, UNESCO has proposed its AI Competency Framework, which acts 
as a guide for AI use and misuse \cite{Pham2026}. This framework highlights that 
student must possess foundational knowledge to ensure they could oversee and validate 
the AI's output \cite{Pham2026,Lo2023}. Furthermore, it is crucial to prevent GenAI 
from taking away student's opportunity to develop cognitive abilities or even 
social skills \cite{sallai2024approach}. Thus, institutions must be able to maintain 
the use of AI as a collaborative tool to enhance student's intelligence rather than replacing it \cite{qian2025pedagogical, sallai2024approach}.

In reality, incorrect uses of GenAI are still commonly found, and often referred to 
as the "productivity paradox" \cite{Wang2024,Bastani2025}. Students tend to 
offload all their cognitive tasks such as planning, organizing, and evaluating in 
exchange for short-term productivity, which consequently 
leads to long-term learning loss \cite{Wang2024,Bastani2025, xia2025systematic}. 
Additionally, evidence shows that most students neglect verifying underlying sources 
of the AI's output as they prioritize the convenience of AI summaries \cite{Loughlin2026}. 
Consequently, the work produced by these students who over-rely on AI are 
generic and lower-quality \cite{sallai2024approach}. In parallel, this leads to a decline 
in their academic performance and memory \cite{Abbas2024}. This proves that unless the AI 
is pedagogically designed to not give out direct answers that can be copied by the students, 
GenAI cannot be a supportive learning partner \cite{xia2025systematic}. Hence, to mitigate this 
problem, educators must provide assessments that enforces students to document their work process, not just 
the final output (e.g., oral examination or AI-output documentation) \cite{Alshumaimeri2024, sallai2024approach}.
Furthermore, to ensure GenAI fosters students' higher-order thinking and creativity, 
institutions must look to combine traditional pedagogy with AI-assisted learning \cite{qian2025pedagogical,prather2024beyond}. 
Thus, pursuing a human-centric GenAI model that acts as a cognitive partner rather than 
replacement is crucial \cite{qian2025pedagogical}.


\subsubsection{Socratic AI versus Direct AI}

As previously mentioned, without proper guardrails, 
GenAI outputs direct answers, posing the risk of cognitive offloading \cite{xia2025systematic, qian2025pedagogical}. 
To prevent this, Socratic chatbots are designed using prompt engineering and guardrails 
to encourage cognitive engagement 
by offering hints and guidance rather than direct answers \cite{Yazici2024,Hilliger2025}.
As the name suggests, the design of Socratic AI chatbots originated from the Socratic teaching method \cite{Jabbour2025}. 
The method itself was originally designed to doubt all assumptions using questions and dialogues, 
forcing students to construct their own knowledge \cite{Wazan2026}. 
This technique has proven to be effective in improving students' learning outcomes, 
increasing their learning gains by 59\% in the context of coding comprehension \cite{Tamang2021}. 
In addition, a comparative study of Socratic AI versus direct AI shows that 
Socratic AI is able to improve learning gains by 127\% with no learning loss when the AI is removed \cite{Bastani2025}.
In comparison, the default model shows a learning loss of 17\% when the AI is removed \cite{Bastani2025}.
Open-ended questions and reflective prompts have been proven to be more useful in 
promoting self-regulated learning, as agreed by 85\% of educators \cite{Hilliger2026}.
This is due to the back-and-forth interaction that encourages students to 
verbalize their reasoning and correct any misconceptions, in comparison 
to the default model that gives direct solutions and deprives students of cognitive effort \cite{Ding2024}.
Thus, it can be acknowledged that the design of the AI interaction is more important 
than the model's capabilities in improving learning outcomes \cite{Bastani2025}. 

Additionally, the convincing tone of default AI models can lead to 
users believing that every response is true, 
despite the inaccuracies caused by hallucinations \cite{sallai2024approach,Jivtode2024}. 
Commonly referred to as the illusion of truth, this characteristic that most AIs possess 
can lead users to overlook hallucinations, which consequently leads to 
errors in their final output \cite{sallai2024approach,Loughlin2026}.
Subsequently, users' confidence tends to align with the AI-expressed confidence, 
further imposing the danger of this illusion \cite{Li2025}. 
Moreover, the authoritative tone of the AI leads students to become over-reliant on the AI, 
which has been proven to limit creativity and critical thinking, as well as 
social and emotional development \cite{KimetAl2026, Li2025}. 
Furthermore, being over-reliant on the AI leads to a lack 
of face-to-face interaction, which negatively impacts students' interpersonal skills and 
leads to a higher risk of social anxiety and isolation \cite{KlimovaPikhart2025}.

To mitigate this, Lo et al. proposed the CLEAR framework, which stands for 
Critical, Reflective, Adaptive, Explainable, and Responsible \cite{Lo2023}. 
This framework encourages students to critically analyze the AI's output, 
reflect on their own learning, adapt to feedback, 
explain the reasoning behind their answers, and be responsible for the final output \cite{Lo2023}.
However, to be able to apply this framework, students must possess foundational 
knowledge of the topic as well as AI literacy to 
critically evaluate the AI's output \cite{Lo2023}. 
Simply flagging whether the content is fake is not effective, as 
students must develop internal critical thinking to succeed \cite{Duelen2024}. 
A study comparing traditional study methods, 
studying with direct AI, and studying with Socratic AI approaches 
found that users who studied with direct AI showed a learning loss of 17\% when the AI was removed, 
while users who studied with the traditional method and Socratic AI showed no learning loss \cite{Bastani2025}.
This further proves that Socratic AI chatbots go beyond fact-checking, 
as they allow students to recognize cognitive biases and build emotional triggers, 
encouraging them to become more cognitively active \cite{Duelen2024}.

Although effective, Socratic chatbots are not a universal solution, especially to 
learners with low AI literacy that finds the interaction characteristic of the chatbot frustrating \cite{Jin2025}.
Moreover, when compared to the default direct AI in the context of coding, students 
assigned with the Socratic AI actually performed worse (less efficient and higher error ratio) \cite{Liding2026}. 
Subsequently, there are no evidence of long-term learning outcomes found, 
meaning the domain and tasks where the AI is used also matters \cite{Liding2026}. 
This study concludes the importance of high AI literacy in using GenAI tools 
effectively \cite{Jin2025, KimetAl2026}. This is further supported by studies that finds 
students with higher AI literacy can steer the AI to output deeper insights, in comparison, 
students with lower AI literacy tend to revert to simple queries \cite{Jin2025,KimetAl2026}. 
However, as previously mentioned, each student has their own unique needs \cite{Jabbour2025}. 
Thus, designing other human-centric models should be pursued to cater to the various learning styles and unique needs of each students \cite{qian2025pedagogical}.


\subsection{Various AI Learning Strategies}
Beyond the Socratic tutoring style, there are actually various other interaction styles 
that students can engage with. Each style possesses its own benefits and risks, as shown in Table \ref{tab:ai-styles} \cite{Mollick2023}.

In supporting the various AI tutoring styles, the ICAP framework \cite{Chi2014} provides a robust categorization of 
various levels of cognitive engagement, from passive to interactive:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Interactive: Collaborative learning by building on each other's ideas (e.g., debate).   
    \item Constructive: Generating new information beyond the materials provided (e.g., mind mapping).  
    \item Active: Learning using physical manipulation without generating anything new (e.g., highlighting). 
    \item Passive: Receiving information without any physical engagement (e.g., listening to a lecture).
\end{enumerate}
The ICAP framework has shown that as students move higher in the hierarchy from 
passive to interactive, learners experience an increase in learning due to 
the knowledge-change processes \cite{Chi2014}. Drawing upon the different ways 
students can engage with AI and its benefits, as well as the validity 
of the ICAP framework, this study proposes to compare three different AI tutoring interactions:
direct-answer, Socratic, and reverse-tutoring. The reverse-tutoring approach is chosen 
due to its potential in fostering active learning, which has been proven to increase 
learning outcomes \cite{Chi2014}.

\subsubsection{Reverse-tutoring (The Protégé Effect)}
"While we teach, we learn" is a famous quote by the Roman philosopher Seneca \cite{Wong2026}. 
Beyond the quote, decades of research have shown that learning-by-teaching 
has proven effective in improving one’s knowledge of a topic \cite{Wong2026,Kobayashi2024}. 
The beneficial learning outcomes of learning-by-teaching 
are driven by socio-psychological mechanisms 
whereby being assigned a teacher role increases students' agency, 
consequently boosting their cognitive engagement and outcomes \cite{Wong2026,Okita2013}. 
This phenomenon is known as the "protégé effect," where students tend to learn more 
as they know they must teach \cite{Okita2013}. 
The phenomenon is further supported by Fiorella and Logan's study, 
which found that the process of reverse-tutoring 
involved two mechanisms: generative processing and metacognitive monitoring \cite{Fiorella2023}. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Generative processing:} Learners generate new information beyond the materials provided and draw relationships between them. This process is driven by the learner's own understanding and knowledge.
    \item \textbf{Metacognitive monitoring:} As learners teach the material, they constantly monitor their own understanding, detecting explanation gaps.
\end{itemize}
The combination of these two mechanisms shifts learners' knowledge 
from surface-level understanding to a flexible mastery of the material \cite{Fiorella2023}. 
These processes are aligned with the interactive level of engagement from the ICAP framework, as 
students are able to reflect on their own understanding and build upon knowledge gaps 
as they teach \cite{Chi2014,Fiorella2023}. Additionally, a comparative study by 
Kobayashi found that students who were explicitly 
told they were going to teach performed twice as well as those who were not told \cite{Kobayashi2024}. 
Moreover, students who later actually taught 
performed even better than those in preparation-only conditions, 
indicating that the act of communicating knowledge 
possesses a unique cognitive benefit beyond preparation alone \cite{Kobayashi2024}.

One of the most frequently cited teachable agents (TA) is Betty's Brain (2007), a pedagogical agent 
built to foster reasoning for middle school students in the subject of biology \cite{BettyBrain2007}. 
The study shows that the presence of the TA was able to absorb the blame for errors, 
allowing students to acknowledge their mistakes while protecting their ego \cite{BettyBrain2007}. 
Additionally, the TA's presence was incredibly beneficial for low-achieving students, who 
were able to score equal to the high-achieving students on the hardest questions \cite{Silvervarg2021,BettyBrain2007}. 
Moreover, the benefits of TAs persisted even after the technology was removed, drawing similarities 
to Socratic AI, where learning outcomes are retained even after the AI is removed \cite{Chin2010,Bastani2025}.

Putting the learning-by-teaching theory into the context of GenAI, 
we reverse the roles of the student and the AI by having the student teach the AI as a naive tutee \cite{Mollick2023}. 
This can be done by designing the personality of the chatbot to be curious and open-minded \cite{Lyu2025}.
By doing so, students are driven to give higher-order explanations and engage in more interactive exchanges \cite{Lyu2025}. 
The AI should act as a recursive feedback tutor that observes what the student has taught it \cite{Okita2013}. 
A study by Jin et al. developed a TA of their own called AlgoBo, which acts 
as a struggling novice in the subject of coding \cite{Jin2024}. AlgoBo is prompt-engineered to 
have strict knowledge of only documents within the chat, and acts as either a help-receiver or 
an active "questioner" that asks why and how questions to the student \cite{Jin2024}. 
This strategy encourages students to think of edge cases and misconceptions, just as denoted by 
the protégé effect \cite{Fiorella2023,Jin2024}.
Moreover, despite prompting students to produce deeper conversations, 
there was no increase in self-reported cognitive load \cite{Jin2024}. 

It is important to note that the personality of these AIs matters \cite{Lyu2025}. 
If the AI fails to emphasize curiosity by showing agreeable and extroverted behavior, 
it may lead to shallow and repetitive exchanges \cite{Lyu2025}. 
Even though it may lead to an increase in interaction volume, the interaction is not driven 
by higher-order engagement \cite{Lyu2025}. 
Therefore, reverse-tutoring is not a one-size-fits-all solution, 
and it depends on the TA's persona design \cite{Lyu2025}.

\subsection{Confidence Calibration}

In the context of human-AI interaction, confidence calibration commonly refers 
to the level of trust a user puts on the AI's output compared to how 
accurate it actually is \cite{Chong2022}. However, the primary definition of 
confidence calibration is defined as how well a person’s subjective sense of 
certainty matches their actual correctness \cite{Chong2022}.
As previously mentioned, AI chatbots possess the illusion of truth, 
leading to users falsely believing the AI's output without 
double-checking the sources, 
causing miscalibration \cite{Berkowitz2026,sallai2024approach}. 
A study by Zhang et al. further proves this as it shows that 
the more confident the AI seems to act, the more the users tend 
to rely on it, consequently more hallucinations are overlooked \cite{Zhang2020Calibration}. 
This poses to be dangerous, especially to lower AI literacy users who 
would often skip evaluating the AI's output. Moreover, they could even 
end up being manipulated and over-rely on the AI due to the confident tone \cite{KimetAl2026}. 
Furthermore, when they finally realize the mistake, users would also tend to 
blame themselves, which leads to destroying their own self-efficacy, and 
eventually cause them to lean harder on the AI \cite{Chong2022}. 
Upon analyzing the danger miscalibration poses, 
confidence calibration should be addressed on both the human-AI aspect of trusting 
the AI's output, but even more importantly on the user's own self-efficacy.

Studies show a consistent pattern where calibration tends to improve when 
the AI shows uncertainty (e.g., asking the user to double-check their output) \cite{Ma2024Calibration,Berkowitz2026}. 
By having this friction, users are urged to fact-check with their own fundamental understanding or 
external sources, which results in a deeper and more accurate final output \cite{Berkowitz2026}. 
Thus, designing an AI system that fosters uncertainty and prompts the user
to fact-check with their own knowledge is crucial for achieving better calibration \cite{Berkowitz2026}.
Additionally, literacy plays a crucial role in the interaction, as users with low literacy
may find the uncertainty friction overwhelming \cite{Jin2025}. Hence, AI usage education,
such as the CLEAR framework \cite{Lo2023}, is crucial in making sure users are able to
understand the uncertainty and fact-check with their own knowledge.

In the scope of this study, the different designs of AI tutoring styles 
will be compared in terms of their calibration performance 
to determine which tutoring style is most effective in fostering 
better calibration. The default direct-answer AI risks strong confidence inflation 
due to the lack of uncertainty friction, leading to overreliance on the AI's output \cite{Bastani2025}. 
Socratic AI may improve calibration by encouraging self-explanation and error checking, 
but may raise frustration \cite{Jin2025,Bastani2025}. Reverse-tutoring 
can surface knowledge gaps and reduce the illusion of competence through 
metacognitive monitoring \cite{Fiorella2023}. Finally, the interaction between 
calibration and self-efficacy is important to note, as it has been proven to be a 
vicious cycle where bad AI advice can reduce the user's self-efficacy, 
which can then increase future dependence on the AI \cite{Chong2022}. 
Following previous work, using multiple assessments from a pre-test, 
followed by an AI-assisted practice, then to a post-test with the 
AI taken away can be effective in distinguishing short-term productivity 
or completion from actual long-term learning outcomes or retained knowledge \cite{Bastani2025}.


\section{Research Question}
\label{sec:research-question}

This study aims to answer the research question:
\textit{How do (A) different generative AI tutoring styles influence the effectiveness of self-regulated learning;
specifically, how does it affect the learner's (B) learning performance, (C) self-efficacy, and 
(D) confidence calibration?}

The different generative AI tutoring styles examined in this research are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Direct-answer AI tutoring, the default nature of AI chatbots
    \item Socratic tutoring, open-ended questions to prompt self-explanations
    \item Reverse-tutoring, AI acts as a naïve tutee and the learner is assigned to teach it
\end{enumerate}

On the other hand, the outcomes measured in the study are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Learning performance, the objective test scores from the multiple-choice assessments
    \item Self-efficacy, how confident the learner is in their own ability to answer the questions correctly
    \item Confidence calibration, how well the learner's confidence actually matches their actual correctness
\end{enumerate}

Additionally, this study hypothesizes reverse-tutoring will output a better 
performance, stronger self-efficacy, and consequently a higher confidence calibration when 
compared to both Socratic and direct-answer tutoring styles. This is due to the 
positive traits of the protégé effect, where by forcing the learners to teach, they 
are able to build a flexible mastery of the topic by exposing knowledge gaps and 
organising their own knowledge.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Overview}
This research plans to conduct a within-subjects study by utilizing 
the Latin Square Design to investigate the effects of 
different generative AI tutoring styles (direct-answer, Socratic, and reverse-tutoring). 
Participants will experience all three styles to help them learn 
data structures and have their knowledge tested across three phases: 
pre-test before learning with AI, practice-test with AI, and finally a 
post-test after the AI is taken away. In order to measure the overall 
effect of the different AI tutoring styles, the following 3 factors 
will be considered: the objective learning outcome based on their performance 
in the multiple-choice assessments, their self-efficacy measured through 
their predicted correct answers and 5-point Likert scale, as well as 
their confidence calibration using the Brier score. Finally, the collected 
data will be analyzed using Generalized Linear Mixed Models (GLMM) for continuous measures 
and Cumulative Link Mixed Models (CLMM) for ordinal psychological data.


\subsection{Materials}
\subsubsection{Study materials}
Data structures are chosen as the topic for this study \cite{Kazemitabaar2025}. 
There will be 3 sets of subtopics that will be used in the study:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Array sorting algorithms
    \item Linked Lists
    \item Binary Search Trees
\end{enumerate}
Data structures are chosen as the topic for this study because they possess 
a good balance of high-level computational thinking, pattern recognition, and 
algorithm design. Since these topics very in difficulty, we will be counterbalancing 
the order of the tutoring styles and the topics to ensure the experience is balanced.
Furthermore, data structures are a subject that is uncommon to 
the general public, making it a neutral and new topic for the participants, especially 
for those outside of the computer science field.

To ensure a self-paced learning experience, participants will be given a 
material video that explains the topic in detail at 
the start of the study \cite{Kazemitabaar2025}. Then, participants will be given a time limit of 
30 minutes to study using the assigned AI. This time limit is chosen to 
ensure users are given enough time, and not feel too fatigued 
while undergoing all three different tutoring styles.
Whenever they feel ready, they can freely 
start the assessment. The assessment consists of 3 sets of 
5 multiple-choice questions each to measure the participant's 
knowledge on a topic for pre-test, practice-test with AI, 
and post-test when the AI is taken away \cite{Bastani2025}. 
Therefore, a total of 45 distinct questions will be answered by the participant across the three stages.
Furthermore, all participants will be doing the study 
in a lab setting to ensure a controlled environment and 
discourage them from cheating.

\subsubsection{AI tutor prototype}
In creating the interface that the participants will interact with, 
an AI chatting interface will be developed 
using Next.js and the Gemini SDK. Prompt engineering and guardrails 
will be used to design the personality of the AI to ensure 
the interaction matches the expected dialogue pattern \cite{Yazici2024}. 
The prototype will then be pilot tested internally to ensure it is working as expected, as well 
as addressed issues from the previous papers (shallow and repetitive exchanges) \cite{Lyu2025}. 
All three tutoring styles will be given the same temperature and configuration, ensuring 
consistent output accuracy and performance across all three tutoring styles \cite{Lo2023}. 
Additionally, participants will be neutrally briefed on the type of AI tutoring style 
they have been assigned to, along with a guide on how to properly interact with it. 
This ensures the participants understand the expected dialogue pattern.

\subsection{Participants}
This study aims to recruit a minimum of 60 participants, obtained from power analysis 
with a combination of 0.25 effect size, 0.05 alpha, and 0.80 power, along with 
5 predictors (2 from tutoring styles (n -1 rule), AI literacy, programming proficiency, and trust in AI ). In maintaining a diverse sample, participants will be recruited from 
different backgrounds (i.e. gender and age) and across a spectrum of programming proficiency. 
Participants will be recruited through an expression of interest 
form, which will be shared across the university only after the ethics application is approved. 
They will then be given a selection of schedules that they can choose from to participate in the study. 
To ensure efficient use of time and resources, students will be scheduled in 
groups of five for each lab session.

\subsection{Data Collection}
Data will be collected through a series of surveys and questionnaires from onboarding 
to the end of the study. Participants’ demographics, background, AI literacy, and level of 
trust in AI will be collected in the expression of interest during the recruitment process. 
Additionally, participants will be asked to fill out a self-report programming proficiency questionnaire 
to measure their experience. The questionnaires consist of 5-point Likert scale questions 
regarding their self-estimation of their programming experience and logical paradigm knowledge \cite{Feigenspan2012}.

All participants' names will be replaced with a code number to ensure anonymity. 
Regarding the study itself, three sets of five multiple-choice questions will be used to assess the participants’ 
objective learning performance across the different stages of the study (pre-test after the initial briefing, 
practice-test after learning with AI, and post-test with the AI taken away). 
In parallel, a questionnaire will be given after each assessment to measure 
participants’ confidence levels in their self-efficacy and predicted correct answers. 
The data will be collected using a 5-point Likert scale to measure how confident they are feeling, 
and also an option from 1 to 5 for their predicted correct answers, where both data will be collected via Qualtrics.
Since the participants 
will be participating in a within-subject study, they will be invited to take three sessions 
to experience all three tutoring styles. Therefore, this data collection process will be repeated 
three times, totaling nine sets of assessments and confidence questionnaires. Additionally, the duration 
that each participant spends during their learning sessions and assessments, as well as the chat logs, will 
be collected for analysis and comparison between different styles. All three tutoring styles 
will be evaluated in one lab session to avoid participants doing independent learning outside of the study. 
To avoid fatigue and burnout, participants will be given a break between each session. Furthermore, participants 
will be compensated hourly, and participant will receive a bonus based 
on their performance in the study to ensure they are motivated to perform their best.

\subsection{Dependent and Independent Variables}

Variables are defined consistently with the research question and hypotheses in section~\ref{sec:research-question}.

The \textbf{independent variable} for this study is the different \emph{tutoring styles} that will be 
manipulated at three levels: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item direct-answer tutoring as the control, 
    \item Socratic guidance, 
    \item reverse tutoring (learning-by-teaching). 
\end{enumerate}
Each participant will experience all three styles in a Latin square randomization 
order to ensure a balanced experience among all three styles. 
Moreover, the study is conducted in a within-subjects design to observe 
how the same participant performs across 
different tutoring styles and to achieve stronger statistical power.


On the other hand, the \textbf{dependent variables} are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Learning performance.} Objective scores retrieved from the multiple-choice assessments, 
  across the pre-test, practice-test with AI, and post-test without AI.
  \item \emph{Self-efficacy.} Scores from a self-efficacy 
  questionnaire given to the user after every assessment, capturing confidence 
  on a 5-point Likert scale, and the number of predicted correct answers.
  \item \emph{Confidence calibration.} Alignment between self-rated confidence 
  and objective correctness. This is computed from the item-level confidence ratings 
  and actual learning performance. 
\end{itemize}

The \textbf{covariates} included in this study are the participants' previous programming proficiency, AI literacy, 
and their propensity towards AI, which are drawn from previous studies \cite{Bastani2025,Jin2025,KimetAl2026}.
To capture the participants' AI literacy, 
the Meta AI Literacy Scale (MAILS) \cite{MAILS2023} modular framework will be used. This 
framework consists of a number of questionnaires with answers on a scale of 1 to 10. Then, to 
capture the participants' trust in and propensity toward AI, the Trust in Automation (TiA) 
questionnaire will be used, which is a widely used set of questionnaires in the human-computer interaction field 
to measure the dynamics between humans and automated systems \cite{TiA}. Alongside these, 
the participants' previous programming proficiency will be collected through 
a self-report questionnaire \cite{Feigenspan2012}.

\subsection{Data Analysis}
After all the studies have been conducted, the raw data collected from the 
platform logs, assessments, and questionnaires will be processed to construct the 
primary dependent variables.
The objective learning performance will be calculated based on the 
sum of the correct answers on the 5-item multiple-choice questions. Then, 
interaction time will be extracted from the platform logs to measure the duration 
each participant spent interacting with the AI to study the topic before proceeding to 
complete the assessment.
In order to measure the confidence calibration, calculation of the 
Brier Score \cite{Brier2025} will be used for each participant. 
This method is a proper scoring rule which is used to measure the 
accuracy of probabilistic prediction to quantify the gap between perceived confidence 
and the absolute outcome \cite{Brier2025}. In this study, the Brier Score will be 
calculated by comparing the participant's 
predicted correct answers with the actual correct answers.

The Brier score ($BS$) for confidence calibration is formally defined as:
\begin{equation}
BS = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{t=1}^{N} (f_t - o_t)^2
\end{equation}
where $f_t$ represents participants' predicted correct answer count out of 5, 
and $o_t$ represents the actual correct answers. 
In order to equally measure the confidence calibration, we will be utilizing the Brier score. 
A student who predicts 4/5 and scores 2/5 (overconfident) will receive the same score as 
a student who predicts 2/5 and scores 4/5 (underconfident). Hence, the magnitude of the 
miscalibration is measured as opposed to the direction.

Due to the Latin Square design study, each participant will 
experience all three tutoring styles, meaning 
their data points will be intrinsically linked. 
For example, a learner who is naturally 
quick may have higher scores across all three settings, 
and this is because of their baseline ability, not 
the influence of the style they are interacting with.

In addressing this dependency, a mixed-effects model 
will be utilized over a traditional statistical test. This is due 
to the characteristic of standard statistical tests that assume 
each data point came from an independent individual, like the 
standard ANOVAs. Therefore, we employ a mixed-effects model to 
differentiate factors that are within-participant (e.g. AI tutoring style) 
and factors that are between them (e.g. individual ability). 
Subsequently, participants' ID will be included as a random effect, 
allowing the model to account for their unique cognitive abilities. 
By doing so, we are able to accurately isolate the different AI 
tutoring styles' impact on the user's learning outcome \cite{Baayen2008}.


Our analysis will be split into two mixed-models: 
\vspace{-0.2em}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[] \textbf{1. Generalized Linear Mixed Models (GLMM):} 
    This model will be used to analyze objective data, such as 
    the actual learning outcome from the multiple-choice assessments, 
    and the Brier score (confidence calibration).
    \vspace{0.2em}
    \item[] \textbf{2. Cumulative Link Mixed Models (CLMM):} 
    On the other hand, this model will be used to analyze the ordinal psychological 
    data points, such as the user's self-efficacy scores from the Likert scale. 
    This is done to prevent any statistical errors due to the fact that 
    human emotions cannot be treated the same as hard numbers.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{-0.5em}

Additionally, to prevent any skew in the assessment data, the participants' 
prior programming proficiency, their AI literacy, and their trust in AI will be 
included as covariates in the models. Furthermore,  
a thematic analysis will be conducted on their interaction logs. 
This is done to identify any 
key themes and patterns in participants' interactions with the AI, 
both positive and negative (e.g., topic mastery, frustration, confusion, etc.).

For each outcome (test score, Brier score, or Likert 
self-efficacy via a CLMM), we fit the same structure: the tutoring 
style in that session, the participant's MAILS, TiA, and self-reported programming proficiency, 
with a random intercept per person since everyone will be completing all three styles.
\begin{equation}
  Y_{ij}
  = \beta_0
  + \beta_1\,\mathrm{Style}_{j}
  + \beta_2\,\mathrm{Literacy}_{i}
  + \beta_3\,\mathrm{TiA}_{i}
  + \beta_4\,\mathrm{ProgExp}_{i}
  + u_i
  + \varepsilon_{ij},
  \label{eq:mixed-model}
\end{equation}

Here $i$ represents the index for participants and $j$ represents the index for their Latin-square sessions. 
Then $\beta_0$ is the intercept, $\beta_1$ captures tutoring style (direct-answer, Socratic, reverse tutoring), 
$\beta_2$--$\beta_4$ capture the three covariates, $u_i$ is the person-specific baseline shift, 
and $\varepsilon_{ij}$ is the error left over for that person-session.


\subsection{Ethics}
This study commits to upholding the principles of conducting research involving humans by 
following ethical guidelines\cite{EthicsGuide}. 
Importantly, the study will commence only after 
the ethics application is approved by the University of Melbourne. 
In following ethical principles, safety of the participants will be 
respected and ensured, breaks are provided in between sessions to avoid any discomfort. 
All participants are also voluntary and 
free from any coercion. 
Before the study begins, participants will be given a consent form with 
a plain language explanation of the study's description, goals, risks, and expected time commitment.

In order to protect participants' privacy, 
all names will be anonymized and their data will be stored securely in a 
password-protected database. 
Finally, participants are free to withdraw from the study at any during the study time without penalty, with their data 
being erased from the database.

\section{Conclusion and contribution}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This study examines effectiveness by measuring participants' self-calibrated confidence, actual objective
learning performance, and the confidence calibration between the two. We plan to uncover the illusion of competence \cite{Bastani2025}
by using the learning-by-teaching AI tutoring style to see whether the protégé effect could mitigate
the illusion and, in parallel, positively impact the user's self-efficacy and learning outcome. This is done by
conducting a within-subject study across three different tutoring styles 
(direct-answer, Socratic, and learning-by-teaching),
where students are tasked to learn data structures using the assigned AI to be
assessed with and without the AI. By doing so, we will be able to analyze which tutoring style
best supports self-efficacy and learning outcomes.

This research contributes to the field of Human-AI interaction by addressing the research gap in
the design of human-centric AI learning tools \cite{qian2025pedagogical}. As found in previous studies,
especially on the exploration of Socratic chatbots, the design of these chatbots is able to increase
users' learning outcomes \cite{Duelen2024,Jabbour2025}. However, beyond Socratic chatbots,
there is a lack of research on the design of other AI tutoring styles.
By introducing the learning-by-teaching AI, we are able to explore a new interaction design and also compare
the effectiveness of another AI learning tool. Furthermore, this study seeks to evaluate whether the 
protégé effect could be translated into human-AI interaction from the
traditional human-to-human medium. Ultimately, future AI learning platforms will be able to utilize
our research findings to design more effective and human-centric AI learning tools.
